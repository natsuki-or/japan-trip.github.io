Palaeolithic Era

Pre-state
Japan
Mobile hunter-gatherer communities using chipped stone tools; earliest signs of ritual belief.
Britain & Europe

Period
c.14,000 BCE – c.10th c. BCE
Jōmon Period
縄文時代
Clan-based
Japan
Warming climate leads to settled pit-dwelling villages and shell middens (kaizuka). One of the earliest pottery cultures in the world.
Culture & Religion
Animist beliefs; clay figurines (dogū) for ritual use; highly decorative flame-style pottery (kaen-doki).
Britain & Europe
Stone Age → Stonehenge era. Settlement without agriculture; Britain develops megalithic ritual monuments.
Period
c.10th c. BCE – c.250 CE
Yayoi Period
弥生時代
Proto-kingdoms
Japan
Wet-rice farming and bronze & iron tools arrive from the continent. Surplus creates wealth gaps and social hierarchy; small kingdoms (kuni) emerge. Queen Himiko of Yamatai first appears in Chinese records.
Culture & Religion
Agricultural ritual using bronze bells (dōtaku) and swords. Thin, utilitarian Yayoi pottery replaces ornate Jōmon styles.
Britain & Europe
Iron Age Britain → Roman invasion (43 CE). Introduction of metals and organised warfare mirrors Japan's Yayoi transition.
Period
c. 250–593 CE
Kofun Period
古墳時代
Imperial (Yamato)
Japan
Keyhole-shaped burial mounds (kofun) mark elite power. The Yamato court gradually unifies the archipelago. Artisans (toraijin) emigrate from Korea & China, bringing new crafts and writing.
Culture & Religion
Elaborate funerary rites; terracotta figurines (haniwa) surround tombs. Continental spirit-beliefs (shinsen shisō) flow in.
Britain & Europe
Anglo-Saxon Heptarchy forms after Roman withdrawal. Parallel emergence of warrior-aristocrat burial mounds (e.g. Sutton Hoo).
Period
593–710 CE
Asuka Period
飛鳥時代
Emperor + Regent
Key figures: Prince Shōtoku (regent, 17-Article Constitution), Taika Reform 645, Taihō Code 701 (Tang-style law)
Japan
First centralised state modelled on Tang China. Aristocratic clans (uji) consolidated under imperial authority. The Taihō legal code establishes ministries, provinces, and a tax system.
Culture & Religion
Buddhism officially adopted (538 or 552 CE); Hōryū-ji temple built — world's oldest surviving wooden structure. Asuka sculpture fuses Korean and Chinese styles (Asuka Culture).
Britain & Europe
Christianity spreads through Britain (Synod of Whitby 664). Both islands absorb a continental world religion that reshapes their entire social fabric simultaneously.
Period
710–794 CE
Nara Period
奈良時代
Emperor
Japan
Capital moved to Nara — first permanent city. Regular embassies (kentōshi) to Tang China import culture wholesale. Emperor Shōmu sponsors massive state Buddhism.
Culture & Religion
Great Buddha of Tōdai-ji cast (752); provincial temples (kokubunji) built nationwide. Cosmopolitan Tenpyō Culture. Chronicles Kojiki (712) & Nihon Shoki (720) compiled.
Britain & Europe
Carolingian Renaissance begins. Both societies absorbing a high continental civilisation to legitimise power and organise the state.
Period
794–1185 CE
Heian Period
平安時代
Emperor → Regents → Cloistered
Key figures: Fujiwara clan (regents), Murasaki Shikibu (The Tale of Genji), Sei Shōnagon (The Pillow Book)
Japan
Capital at Kyoto. Real power held by Fujiwara regents (sesshō / kampaku) early on, then by retired emperors (insei). Late period: Taira & Minamoto clans fight for supremacy (Genpei War 1180–85).
Culture & Religion
After missions to China end, a uniquely Japanese aesthetic blooms: yamato-e painting, hiragana script, mono no aware sensibility. Pure Land Buddhism (Jōdo) spreads. World's first novel written.
Britain & Europe
Viking raids → Norman Conquest (1066). Power shifts from monarch to barons; courtly literature and chivalric culture flourish — a parallel to Heian aristocratic refinement.
Period
1185–1333
Kamakura Shogunate
鎌倉時代
Shogun (Warrior)
Key figures: Minamoto no Yoritomo (1st Shogun), Hōjō regents — Mongol Invasions: 1274 & 1281 repelled (kamikaze winds)
Japan
Japan's first warrior government (bakufu) at Kamakura. Emperor remains symbolic in Kyoto; real rule is by the shogun, then the Hōjō regent-family. Feudal land grants cement samurai loyalty.
Culture & Religion
New accessible Buddhist sects: Zen, Jōdo, Nichiren — spread to warriors and commoners. Vigorous realistic sculpture; dramatic illustrated scrolls (emakimono).
Britain & Europe
Magna Carta (1215); High Middle Ages. Knight-based feudalism mirrors samurai culture: land for loyalty, honour codes, and a warrior class rising above old aristocracy.
Period
1336–1573
Muromachi (Ashikaga) Shogunate
室町時代
Shogun → Warlords
Includes: Nanbokuchō (dual courts), Ōnin War (1467), and the Sengoku (Warring States) era of gekokujō — "the low overthrowing the high"
Japan
Ashikaga shoguns rule from Kyoto but authority fractures. The Ōnin War (1467) triggers a century of warlord warfare (Sengoku). Regional lords (daimyō) become autonomous rulers.
Culture & Religion
Zen aesthetics permeate elite culture: Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion), Ginkaku-ji, ink-wash painting (suiboku-ga). Tea ceremony (chanoyu) and Noh theatre — the DNA of classical Japanese arts — take form.
Britain & Europe
Wars of the Roses (1455–85) → Tudor dynasty. Both nations experience prolonged internal civil war as central authority collapses, resolved by new strongmen.
Period
1573–1603
Azuchi–Momoyama Period
安土桃山時代
Unification
Key figures: Oda Nobunaga, Toyotomi Hideyoshi (Taikō Survey & Sword Hunt), Sen no Rikyū (wabi-cha)
Japan
Nobunaga and Hideyoshi reunify Japan by force. Land surveys (taiko kenchi) standardise taxation; sword hunts (katanagari) disarm peasants and rigidify class boundaries. Hideyoshi invades Korea twice (1592, 1597).
Culture & Religion
Christianity and muskets arrive from Portugal (1543). Flamboyant Momoyama culture: gold-screen paintings, grand castles. Simultaneously, Rikyū perfects the austere wabi-cha aesthetic. Nanban (Southern Barbarian) art fusion.
Britain & Europe
Elizabethan England; European Wars of Religion. Gunpowder revolution transforms warfare on both continents. Trade routes expand globally.
Period
1603–1868
Edo Period (Tokugawa Shogunate)
江戸時代
Shogun (265 yrs)
Key figures: Tokugawa Ieyasu (founder), Commodore Perry 1853 (forced opening). Policy of sakoku (isolation) with limited Dutch & Chinese trade at Nagasaki
Japan
Tokugawa shoguns rule from Edo (Tokyo). Rigid four-class system: samurai, farmers, artisans, merchants. ~260 years of domestic peace. Daimyō kept weak via alternate attendance (sankin-kōtai) policy.
Culture & Religion
Christianity banned; Neo-Confucian (Zhu Xi) ideology promoted. Urban merchant culture flourishes: ukiyo-e woodblock prints (Hokusai, Hiroshige), kabuki theatre, haiku poetry. Rising literacy and commercialisation.
Britain & Europe
Shakespeare → Industrial Revolution → early Victorian era. While Britain industrialises and builds a global empire, Japan isolated.
Period
1868–1912
Meiji Period
明治時代
Meiji Emperor
Key events: Meiji Restoration 1868, Meiji Constitution 1889, Sino-Japanese War 1894–95, Russo-Japanese War 1904–05, Anglo-Japanese Alliance 1902
Japan
Western pressure (Perry's Black Ships) collapses the shogunate. The Emperor restored to nominal power; oligarchs drive rapid Westernisation — railways, a conscript army, a constitution, and compulsory education — to resist colonisation.
Culture & Religion
Shinto separated from Buddhism and elevated as state religion. Western art imports alongside the invented tradition of Nihonga (Japanese painting). Japonisme: Japanese aesthetics profoundly influence European Impressionism.
Britain & Europe
Victorian zenith & Industrial Revolution's spread. Japan consciously benchmarks Britain (navy), Germany (army), France (law). The two nations become formal allies in 1902.
Period
1912–1926
Taishō Period
大正時代
Constitutional Monarchy
Japan
"Taishō Democracy": genuine party politics and expanded male suffrage. Japan joins WWI on Allied side; gains Pacific island territories and a permanent seat on the League of Nations Council. Great Kantō Earthquake 1923.
Culture & Religion
Western modernity floods in: jazz, cinema, department stores, "modern girl" (moga) fashion. Taishō Romanticism in literature. The Mingei (Folk Craft) movement celebrates everyday beauty.
Britain & Europe
WWI & aftermath; Roaring Twenties. Both societies experience democratic optimism alongside economic strain and social upheaval in the wake of total war.
Period
1926–1989
Shōwa Period
昭和時代
Emperor (Military → Democracy)
Key events: Manchuria 1931, Second Sino-Japanese War 1937, Pearl Harbour 1941, Hiroshima & Nagasaki 1945, GHQ Occupation 1945–52, Economic Miracle 1955–73
Japan
Pre-war: military factions seize control; Japan expands across Asia. Defeated 1945; US-led occupation rewrites the constitution (peace clause, Article 9) and demilitarises. Post-war: democratic growth into world's second-largest economy.
Culture & Religion
Wartime: state Shinto and propaganda art dominate. Post-war: freedom of religion restored. Manga and anime emerge; Tezuka Osamu lays modern foundations.
Britain & Europe
WWII → Cold War → Welfare State. Japan and Britain both rebuild post-war; both undergo economic recoveries.
Period
1989–Present
Heisei & Reiwa Periods
平成・令和時代
Constitutional Monarchy
Key events: Bubble burst 1991, Kobe Earthquake 1995, Fukushima disaster 2011, Emperor Akihito abdicates 2019 → Emperor Naruhito (Reiwa era begins)
Japan
Asset bubble collapses (1991) → "Lost Decade(s)" of economic stagnation. Ageing population and falling birth rate pose structural challenges. IT revolution and digital transformation (DX) pursued. World's fourth-largest economy.
Culture & Religion
Contemporary art diversifies. Anime, manga, video games, J-pop — "Cool Japan" — become major cultural exports and global soft power.
Britain & Europe
Post-Cold War → Brexit era. Both nations face demographic ageing and economic uncertainty while remaining cultural exports of global influence.
